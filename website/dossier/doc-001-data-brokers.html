What data brokers actually know about you

You have never spoken to a data broker. A data broker has never asked your permission. And yet, if you live a normal digital life, several of them hold a file on you — assembled from purchases, registrations, public records and app telemetry, then sold to anyone with a budget. This document reviews what has been established on the record.

01 / The inventory, on the record

The most complete official accounting of the industry remains the U.S. Federal Trade Commission's 2014 study of nine brokers, Data Brokers: A Call for Transparency and Accountability.[01] Its findings were not estimates — the FTC compelled the companies to describe their own holdings:

  • One broker, Acxiom, held data on about 700 million consumers worldwide, with over 3,000 data segments for nearly every U.S. consumer.
  • Another held information on more than 1.4 billion consumer transactions and over 700 billion data elements.
  • Segments offered for sale included categories such as "Expectant Parent," "Diabetes Interest," and "Cholesterol Focus."

Note what those last three have in common: they are health inferences, drawn without a doctor, sold without a consent form.

3,000+
Data segments held on nearly every U.S. consumer by a single broker — per the FTC's own 2014 report, from the broker's own disclosures.

02 / How cheap your inner life is

If the FTC report feels dated, the market has only matured. In 2023, a researcher at Duke University's Sanford School of Public Policy contacted 37 data brokers asking to buy Americans' mental-health data. 26 responded; 11 were willing and able to sell — lists covering depression, anxiety, OCD, bipolar disorder and stroke, in some cases alongside race, ethnicity and whether children live in the home. One firm quoted $275 for 5,000 aggregated mental-health records; others offered yearly subscription access for $75,000–$100,000.[02]

There was no hacking involved. The researcher sent emails and asked. That is the point: this is a functioning retail market, not a black one.

03 / "Anonymous" until it isn't

Case file · 2021

A Catholic newsletter obtained commercially available location data originating from the dating app Grindr and used it to identify a senior priest, Monsignor Jeffrey Burrill, by correlating an "anonymized" device's movements with his residence and workplaces. He resigned within days.[03] No warrant, no subpoena — a purchase.

The Burrill case matters because it demonstrates the whole pipeline in one incident: an app leaks a signal, a broker packages it, a buyer re-identifies a person. Every claim of "anonymized data" should be read against it — and against the re-identification research collected in DOC 005.

04 / Why this file exists in a dossier about prompts

A broker's file is a mosaic. No single tile is dramatic: a magazine subscription here, a loyalty card there, a location ping, a warranty registration. The profile emerges from the joins — small facts about the same person, correlated across sources.

Prompts to AI systems are becoming an unusually rich tile. People type things into a chat box they would never put on a form: health worries, finances, relationship problems, their employer's internal documents. Each prompt that leaves your machine with your name, address or case details in it is a candidate tile for someone's mosaic — stored on infrastructure you don't control, governed by policies you don't set, retained for windows you don't choose (see DOC 002).

You cannot practically stop brokers from trading in your past. You can stop feeding the mosaic going forward. A prompt that leaves your machine reading ⟦PERSON_1⟧, ⟦ADDRESS_1⟧, ⟦HEALTH_1⟧ contributes nothing to anyone's file — there is no join left to make.
