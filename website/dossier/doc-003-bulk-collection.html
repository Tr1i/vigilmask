A short history of bulk collection

Nothing below is speculation. Every program in this file has been confirmed by official documents, court rulings, parliamentary inquiries or the operators themselves. The pattern across five decades is consistent: where communications concentrate, collection follows — and each revelation changed less than it appeared to.

01 / The record, in order

1970–2018 · Operation Rubicon

For decades, Swiss encryption vendor Crypto AG sold "secure" cipher machines to governments worldwide — while secretly owned by the CIA (initially with Germany's BND). The devices were rigged; the communications of more than 100 countries were read by the agencies their buyers thought they were locked against. Confirmed in 2020 by The Washington Post and ZDF from the CIA's own internal history.[01]

2001 · ECHELON

A European Parliament inquiry formally concluded that a global system for intercepting private and commercial communications — operated by the U.S., U.K., Canada, Australia and New Zealand — existed and was being used, and warned European citizens and businesses accordingly.[02]

2006 · Room 641A

AT&T technician Mark Klein disclosed that the NSA had installed fiber-optic splitters in AT&T's San Francisco switching facility, copying backbone internet traffic wholesale into a secure room. Not targeted interception — a duplicate of the pipe.[03]

2013 · The Snowden files

Leaked internal documents confirmed, among others: bulk collection of Americans' telephone metadata under Section 215 of the PATRIOT Act; PRISM, structured access to user data held by major internet companies; and GCHQ's Tempora, buffering transatlantic cable traffic. The scale was no longer inferable — it was printed.[04]

02 / What changed

  • In 2015 a U.S. federal appeals court held that the bulk phone-metadata program exceeded what the statute authorized (ACLU v. Clapper), and Congress ended that specific program with the USA FREEDOM Act.[05] A second appeals court reached a similar conclusion in 2020 (United States v. Moalin).
  • In Europe, the Court of Justice struck down the Data Retention Directive (2014) and twice invalidated EU–US data-transfer frameworks over U.S. surveillance law — Safe Harbor in 2015 and Privacy Shield in 2020 (Schrems II).[06]
  • End-to-end encryption went mainstream: the fraction of web and messaging traffic opaque to passive interception rose dramatically after 2013.

03 / What quietly didn't

The corrections were narrower than the headlines. One program ended; the architecture did not:

  • Section 702 — the legal basis for large-scale collection of communications from U.S. providers, including the upstream and PRISM-style access documented in 2013 — was reauthorized by Congress in April 2024, with a broadened definition of which service providers can be compelled to assist.[07]
  • Court rulings addressed domestic metadata; bulk collection of foreigners' communications was never the subject of the remedies.
  • And a structural shift did the quiet work: as DOC 001 documents, agencies increasingly need not compel what they can purchase from the commercial data market.
100+
Countries whose "encrypted" government communications were read through Crypto AG — a reminder that trust in an intermediary is a single point of failure, for states as for individuals.

04 / The inference for the AI era

This history licenses exactly one prediction, and it is a modest one: large concentrations of intimate text attract institutional attention. Cloud AI providers are accumulating precisely such a concentration — billions of prompts containing health details, legal matters, finances and identities, indexed by account. Whether access eventually arrives via legal process (see DOC 002), commercial purchase, or breach, the record above says: plan for the pipe to be looked at.

The countermeasure is the same one encryption taught: make the observable copy worthless. A prompt stream of ⟦PERSON_1⟧ and ⟦ADDRESS_1⟧ tokens is a pipe not worth tapping.
