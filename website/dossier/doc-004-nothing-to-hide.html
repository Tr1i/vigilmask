"Nothing to hide" doesn't hold up

It is the most common reply to any privacy argument, and it sounds like common sense: if I've done nothing wrong, why would I care who reads my prompts? The reply has been examined carefully — most thoroughly by legal scholar Daniel Solove — and it fails on four independent grounds.[01] Any one of them is sufficient.

01 / It mistakes what privacy is

The argument assumes privacy exists to conceal wrongdoing — so the innocent need none. But you lock the bathroom door, seal envelopes, and keep your salary off your forehead without hiding any crime. Privacy is not secrecy about misdeeds; it is control over context: deciding who gets which facts about you, for what purpose. Solove's survey of the harms makes the point structurally — aggregation, secondary use, exclusion — none of which require you to have done anything wrong. They only require someone else to hold your data.

The aggregation harm is the one this dossier keeps returning to: individually innocent facts combine into something none of them was (see DOC 001). "Nothing to hide" evaluates each fact alone. Files are not read that way.

02 / Surveillance measurably changes the innocent

If watching only bothered wrongdoers, the behavior of ordinary people would not shift when they learn they're observed. It does, and it has been measured:

  • After the June 2013 surveillance revelations, traffic to Wikipedia articles on sensitive security topics dropped roughly 20 percent — with the depressed trend persisting afterward. Nobody banned the articles. People stopped looking things up.[02]
  • A PEN America survey of writers the same year found 1 in 6 had avoided writing or speaking on a topic they believed would subject them to surveillance.[03]
−20%
Decline in views of privacy-sensitive Wikipedia articles after June 2013 — a chilling effect on reading, the most innocent act there is. Penney, Berkeley Technology Law Journal (2016).

This is the cost the argument ignores: surveillance taxes curiosity, speech and inquiry across the whole population, not just among the guilty. What questions would you not ask an AI if a person were reading over your shoulder? That hesitation is the harm, already operating.

03 / The rules change; the data remains

"Nothing to hide" is always evaluated against today's rules. But data outlives rules. A record created lawfully under one regime is inherited by every future regime, market and owner — and reinterpreted by them.

Case file · Nebraska, 2022

Police investigating an illegal abortion obtained a mother and daughter's private Facebook messages by warrant; the messages, in which the two discussed medication, became core evidence and both were convicted. When the messages were written, the pair evidently considered them private chat. The investigators read them as records.[04]

Swap in any axis you like — political opinion, sexuality, religion, immigration status, a health decision. Whether you have something to hide is not a fact about you; it is a function of who is asking, and when. You do not control either variable. You control only what exists to be asked about.

04 / Everyone has something — just not a crime

Medical worries. A salary negotiation. A client's case file. A friend's confidence. An affair survived, a diagnosis private, a visa pending. The centuries-old quip attributed (probably apocryphally) to Cardinal Richelieu — that six lines written by the most honest of men contain something with which to hang him — survives because it names something true: any sufficiently complete record can be read against its author. The defense is not innocence. The defense is incompleteness of the record.

05 / Application

Note what this argument does not require: no villainous provider, no dystopia, no prediction at all beyond "rules and owners change." The conclusion is procedural. Minimize the record at the moment of its creation — which, for AI chat, means the moment before transmission (see the procedure). A placeholder cannot be aggregated, chilled, subpoenaed or reinterpreted. The most honest of men can safely write six lines about ⟦PERSON_1⟧.
