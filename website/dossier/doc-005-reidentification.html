How "anonymous" data gets traced back

"Don't worry — the data is anonymized." That sentence has preceded most of the notable privacy failures of the last thirty years. The reason is mathematical, not moral: you are more unique than you think, and stripping names from a dataset barely reduces that uniqueness. The demonstrations below are the classics of the field, each replicated, none refuted.

01 / Three facts suffice

In 2000, Latanya Sweeney showed that 87% of the U.S. population is uniquely identified by just three attributes: 5-digit ZIP code, gender, and date of birth.[01] She had already made the point unforgettable in 1997: when Massachusetts released "anonymized" state-employee hospital records, she cross-referenced them with the public voter roll — and mailed Governor William Weld his own medical file.

87%
Of Americans uniquely identified by ZIP code + gender + date of birth alone. Sweeney, 2000. No name required — the combination is the name.

02 / The pattern repeats, dataset by dataset

2006 · AOL search logs

AOL published 20 million "anonymized" search queries for research, users replaced by numbers. Within days, New York Times reporters identified user No. 4417749 from her queries alone — Thelma Arnold, 62, of Lilburn, Georgia — and knocked on her door.[02] Searches, like prompts, are self-describing: the content identifies the author.

2008 · Netflix Prize

Netflix released 100 million "anonymized" movie ratings for a public competition. Narayanan and Shmatikov showed that a handful of ratings and approximate dates — obtainable from public IMDb reviews — sufficed to re-identify subscribers, viewing histories and all.[03] After an FTC complaint and a lawsuit, the planned sequel competition was cancelled.

2013–2015 · Movement and money

De Montjoye and colleagues showed that in a mobility dataset of 1.5 million people, four spacetime points — places and hours a phone was seen — uniquely identify 95% of individuals; four purchases do the same for 90% of credit-card users.[04] Where you were on four occasions is your name.

2016 · Australian health records

Australia published a "de-identified" 10% sample of the national medical billing system. University of Melbourne researchers first decrypted provider IDs, then showed patients could be re-identified from known facts about them — including public information about celebrities and politicians. The dataset was withdrawn.[05]

03 / The general theorem

In 2019, researchers at Imperial College London and UCLouvain generalized what the case files suggest: using standard demographic attributes, 99.98% of Americans would be correctly re-identified in any dataset containing 15 such attributes — and they published a model showing that even heavily sampled, incomplete releases don't provide the protection their publishers claim.[06] "We only released a sample" is not a defense; it is a delay.

99.98%
Of Americans correctly re-identifiable from 15 demographic attributes. Rocher, Hendrickx & de Montjoye, Nature Communications, 2019.

04 / What this means for prompts

Now read your own prompts as a re-identification scientist would. "Draft a complaint to my landlord at Bergstraße 14," "summarize this patient note, DOB 03/11/1987," "review this contract for Meridian Logistics GmbH" — each contains multiple quasi-identifiers, embedded in free text, accumulating in one account-linked history. Prompt logs are the AOL dataset with better grammar.

The engineering conclusion the research supports is the one Sweeney reached decades ago: anonymization after collection is a promise; suppression before collection is a property. A placeholder like ⟦ADDRESS_1⟧ carries zero bits of quasi-identifier. There is nothing to join, sample, or model — not because anyone promised, but because the data never existed on the other side. That property is the entire design brief of the redaction procedure.
