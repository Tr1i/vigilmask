What leaks when you paste into a chatbot

The paste shortcut is the most dangerous keybinding in modern office work. Nobody types a client database into a chat window — but pasting a document to "just summarize this" moves it to third-party infrastructure in one gesture. This field guide covers what actually gets pasted, measured; and what has already gone wrong, documented.

01 / The measurements

Security firm Cyberhaven instrumented data flows for 1.6 million knowledge workers at its client companies during ChatGPT's first breakout year. Its findings, published in 2023: 11% of the content employees pasted into ChatGPT was confidential — source code, client data, regulated information — and the average company leaked confidential material to the chatbot hundreds of times per week.[01] Among the incidents it recorded: an executive pasting a firm's annual strategy document to get slides made, and a doctor pasting a patient's name and condition to draft an insurance letter.

02 / Case files

2023 · Samsung semiconductor division

Within weeks of permitting ChatGPT, Samsung logged three separate incidents: an engineer pasted proprietary source code to debug it, another submitted internal meeting notes for summarization, a third uploaded chip test data. The company subsequently banned generative AI tools on corporate devices.[02] None of the three was malicious. All three were Tuesday.

2023 · The provider side fails too

In March 2023 a caching bug caused some ChatGPT users to see titles from other users' chat histories; OpenAI's postmortem acknowledged that payment-related details (name, email, card last-four, expiry) of about 1.2% of Plus subscribers active in a nine-hour window may also have been visible.[03] Italy's data-protection authority temporarily blocked the service weeks later. The bug was ordinary; the lesson is structural — whatever you pasted is included in someone else's incident scope.

2025 · Shared chats, indexed by Google

ChatGPT's share-a-conversation feature briefly offered a "discoverable" option; thousands of shared conversations — some containing deeply personal detail — surfaced in Google search results, and OpenAI withdrew the feature.[04] Data that leaves your machine acquires exposure paths its author never imagined.

03 / Field guide: what people actually paste

From the measured incidents above, the recurring cargo — check it against your own last month of prompts:

  • Client and case material — names, matters, contract terms, deal figures. In many professions this is a confidentiality breach the moment it lands on a third-party server, whatever the provider's policy says.
  • Source code and internal documents — strategy memos, meeting notes, credentials embedded in config files.
  • Medical detail — patient notes, your own symptoms, insurance IDs, dates of birth.
  • Identity anchors — the emails, phone numbers, addresses and card numbers that make the rest of a profile joinable (see DOC 005).

These categories are exactly the entity classes in the ticker on the cover page — that is not a coincidence; the detection engine's target list was derived from what actually leaks.

04 / Why policy is not a fix

The instinctive corporate response — ban the tools — measurably fails: the assistants are too useful, so usage moves to personal phones and home accounts, where no policy reaches. The realistic fix accepts the pasting and changes what the paste contains. Strike the identifiers locally, before transmission; let the model work on placeholders; restore the real values in the reply on your own machine (the three-stage procedure). The executive still gets slides. The doctor still gets a letter. The strategy document and the patient, however, never make the trip.
